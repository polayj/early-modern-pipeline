i could tell you what we have of both forts that is brought to us from other parts of the world; as biskets, both fine and coorse, barrels of meale clofe put up; which comes to us very sweet from england; and holland's of which we make bread, pye-craft, and puddings. and for drink good english beer, french and spanish wines, with others, some from the materas, some from fiall, one of the islands of aores; so we cannot justify compliance of wants, either of bread or drink, and from england, spirits, some of annifeds, some of mint, some of wormwood, &c. and from france, brandy, which is extreme strong, but accounted very wholesome. having given you a just account, as neere as my memory will serve of the bread and drink of this island : the next thing is the feverall fortes of meat we have there; and because hogges flesh is the most general meat, and indeed the best the island affords, i will begin with that, which is (without question) as good, as any can be of that kind: for their feeding being as good, as can grow any where, the flesh must needs be an averable; fruit, the nuts of locuf, pompians of a rare kind, almost as sweet as milions, the bodies of the plantines, and bonanoses, sugar-canes, and mayes, being their dayly food. when we came first upon the island, i perceive the ties they made to hold them, were trees, with the ends lying crofie upon one another, and the inclosure they made, was not large enough to hold the numbers of hogges were in them, with convenient distance to play and flire themselves for their health, and pleafures; so that they were in a manner petterd, and chookt up, with their own fink, which is sure the most noyfome of any other beaft, and by reason of the suns heat much wore; i have fmet the fink of one of those fies down the wind, neer a mile, through all the wood: and the crowding and thrufing them so clofe together, was certainly the caufe of their want of health, which much hindred their growth;
